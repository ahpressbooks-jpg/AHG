Policy Agenda

AHG Does Not React to Policy. AHG Drives It.

Six fights. Every young person between 14 and 26. DFW today. The nation by Year 3.

The Six Fights

The Policy Agenda.

Every position below is non-negotiable. AHG tracks every relevant piece of legislation, responds publicly, and names the officials who vote against the people they claim to represent.

01

Mental Health Infrastructure in Schools

Schools are the primary point of contact for young people in crisis -- and most of them are completely unequipped to respond. AHG demands fully funded, staffed, and structurally integrated mental health infrastructure in every public school in Texas. Not a poster on the wall. Not a hotline number. A system.

We fight for mandated counselor-to-student ratios, on-campus mental health professionals with clinical authority, and crisis intervention protocols that do not begin and end with suspension.

"A school without adequate mental health infrastructure is failing its students. Fund it like that is true -- not like it is optional."
02

Education System Redesign

The American education system was not designed for the students it currently serves. Bell schedules, curricula, and disciplinary systems were built for compliance, not development. AHG fights for a fundamental redesign of how schools operate -- starting with the recognition that students are not inputs in a production model.

We advocate for flexible scheduling, culturally responsive curricula, restorative discipline, project-based learning, and the elimination of zero-tolerance policies that push students out instead of building them up.

"If the system was designed for a different era, stop defending it. Redesign it for the students who are in it right now."
03

Foster Care Transition Reform

In Texas, only 48% of eligible foster youth enroll in extended foster care. The rest age out with no housing plan, no employment pipeline, and no relational safety net. AHG fights for a complete overhaul of how Texas supports young people transitioning out of foster care -- extending meaningful support to age 26, not just paperwork to age 21.

We demand guaranteed transitional housing, dedicated case management with manageable caseloads, educational continuity plans, and workforce onramps that treat foster alumni as assets, not liabilities.

"The state took responsibility for these children. It does not get to walk away when they turn 18."
04

Juvenile Justice Reform

AHG opposes the criminalization of adolescence. The juvenile justice system in Texas disproportionately punishes Black and brown youth for behaviors that white youth are counseled through. We fight for restorative justice models, the elimination of juvenile life without parole, and the end of the school-to-prison pipeline.

We advocate for diversion programs, community-based alternatives to detention, sealing of juvenile records, and investment in prevention rather than punishment.

"A 15-year-old who makes a mistake is not a criminal. They are a child. Build a system that treats them like one."
05

Youth Representation in Civic Institutions

Young people are the subject of almost every major policy decision in America. They are almost never in the room when it is made. AHG fights for the formal inclusion of young people in civic institutions -- not as tokens, not as interns, but as decision-makers with real authority.

We advocate for youth seats on school boards, city councils, and state advisory bodies. We fight for voting access, civic education that is honest about power, and structural pathways into governance for people under 30.

"If you are old enough to be affected by the policy, you are old enough to shape it. Give them the seat."
06

Housing Access for Young Adults

46% of foster care alumni experience homelessness before age 26. That is not a gap in the system. That is the system. AHG fights for guaranteed transitional housing for every young adult aging out of foster care, and for affordable housing policy that recognizes young adults as a distinct population with distinct needs.

We demand expanded housing voucher eligibility, dedicated young adult housing developments, rental assistance programs, and the elimination of policies that make it functionally impossible for a 19-year-old with no credit history and no co-signer to secure stable housing.

"A society that removes children from their homes and then offers them no home at 18 is not a society that has solved anything. It has just transferred the crisis."
